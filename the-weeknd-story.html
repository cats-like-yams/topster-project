The Weeknd -- My Story

My high school years after Covid were basically soundtracked by music. I was obsessed with R&B and hip-hop, constantly doing deep dives into artists and trying to understand what made them resonate so hard with their fans. I knew The Weeknd was everywhere, but honestly, I only knew his radio hits. Then one Friday night, I decided to actually listen to House of Balloons—his first mixtape—while reading my favorite manga. Weird combo, I know, but something just clicked. I don't remember a single thing I read that night, but I remember every feeling those songs gave me. It was like the music was speaking directly to my emotional state.

His voice is hypnotic in a way that's hard to explain, and House of Balloons immediately became my all-time favorite. Now I can't even pick a favorite album of his anymore—I bounce between House of Balloons, Kiss Land, and Dawn FM depending on my mood. What gets me about The Weeknd is how he doesn't shy away from his mess. Songs like "Loft Music," "Life of the Party," and "The Birds Pt. 1" are unapologetically about his toxic, self-destructive side. But then he shows up with albums like My Dear Melancholy, After Hours, and especially Dawn FM where he's actually reflecting, growing, and showing up as a better version of himself. That duality—the rawness paired with the growth—makes him an artist worth paying attention to. It's completely understandable why one of the top artists in the world.

Favorite Albums

  • House of Balloons: The trilogy's start—dark, introspective, and perfect for those late-night drives where you need something that matches your mood.
  • Thursday: The party record, but also kind of the most toxic? It's wild in the best way.
  • Echoes of Silence: The experimental one that actually messes with your head. The "XO/The Host" to "Initiation" transition is legitimately unsettling every time.
  • Kiss Land: More pop-oriented, way more polished R&B. He's vulnerable here about struggling with relationships while his career took off—there's real conflict in these songs.
  • Dawn FM: Honestly underrated by people who sleep on this album. It's a full concept album about him stuck in purgatory, reflecting on all his mistakes, and figuring out why he did what he did. The Jim Carrey monologue at the end about not being afraid of getting hurt? Yeah, that got me emotional. lol
  • Hurry Up Tomorrow: The most synth-heavy, experimental thing he's put out. It's beautiful and vulnerable, but this time it's about his career and legacy—how he wants to be remembered. I relate to it a lot because of all the uncertainty about the future and how much responsibility can drain the joy out of something you love.
